Introduction

Model Predictive Control (MPC) has become one of the most influential paradigms in control for modern robotics, especially in humanoid locomotion. This article provides a structured and mathematically rigorous introduction to MPC, starting from linear systems and moving toward the architectures used in real humanoid robots.

We cover:

  • The classical Model–Plan–Control (MPC) framework in robotics
  • The mathematical foundations of Model Predictive Control
  • A fully worked-out MPC example for a simple robot (a 1D mass)
  • The structure of an MPC used for humanoid locomotion (LIPM + ZMP)
  • Whole-Body Control using Quadratic Programming (WBQP)
  • The complete locomotion architecture used on real humanoids (Atlas, HRP-4, Talos, Digit, etc.)

1. Foundations of Model Predictive Control (MPC)

MPC solves, at each control step, an optimization problem over a finite prediction horizon. Given a discrete-time linear system:

\[ x_{k+1} = A x_k + B u_k , \]

we define a cost function:

\[ J = \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} \left( x_{k+i}^\top Q x_{k+i} + u_{k+i}^\top R u_{k+i} \right) + x_{k+N}^\top P x_{k+N}, \]

and constraints:

\[ x_{k+i} \in \mathcal{X},\quad u_{k+i} \in \mathcal{U}. \]

At time step \(k\), MPC solves:

\[ \begin{aligned} \min_{\{u_k, \dots, u_{k+N-1}\}} \quad & J \\ \text{s.t.} \quad & x_{k+i+1} = A x_{k+i} + B u_{k+i}, \\ & x_{k+i} \in \mathcal{X}, \\ & u_{k+i} \in \mathcal{U}. \end{aligned} \]

Only the first control input \(u_k^{*}\) is applied.
At the next time step, the optimization is solved again (receding horizon).

2. Example: MPC for a Simple 1D Robot (Double Integrator)

We consider a robot moving along a line, with state:

\[ x = \begin{bmatrix} p \\ v \end{bmatrix}, \]

and acceleration input \(a = u\). The continuous-time model is:

\[ \dot{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} x + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} u . \]

2.1 Discretization

Using a sampling period \(T_s = 0.1\,\text{s}\):

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & T_s \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad B = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{T_s^2}{2} \\ T_s \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.005 \\ 0.1 \end{bmatrix}. \]

2.2 Dynamic prediction

For horizon \(N = 3\):

[ \[\begin{aligned} x_1 &= A x_0 + B u_0, \\ x_2 &= A^2 x_0 + A B u_0 + B u_1, \\ x_3 &= A^3 x_0 + A^2 B u_0 + A B u_1 + B u_2. \end{aligned}\]

]

With:

\[ A^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0.2 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad A^3 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0.3 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \]

2.3 Stacked form

We define stacked vectors:

\[ \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} u_0 \\ u_1 \\ u_2 \end{bmatrix}. \]

Then:

\[ \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{A} x_0 + \mathbf{B} \mathbf{u}. \]

Where:

\[ \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} A \\ A^2 \\ A^3 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{B} = \begin{bmatrix} B & 0 & 0 \\ A B & B & 0 \\ A^2 B & A B & B \end{bmatrix}. \]

2.4 MPC cost in quadratic form

Define:

  • \(\mathbf{Q} = \text{diag}(Q, Q, Q)\)
  • \(\mathbf{R} = \text{diag}(R, R, R)\)

Then:

\[ J(\mathbf{u}) = \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{u}^\top H \mathbf{u} + f^\top \mathbf{u} + \text{constant}, \]

with:

\[ H = 2(\mathbf{B}^\top \mathbf{Q} \mathbf{B} + \mathbf{R}), \qquad f = 2\mathbf{B}^\top \mathbf{Q} \mathbf{A} x_0 . \]

This is a Quadratic Program (QP):

\[ \min_{\mathbf{u}} \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{u}^\top H \mathbf{u} + f^\top \mathbf{u}. \]

Constraints (input saturation, position bounds, etc.) yield linear inequalities:

\[ G \mathbf{u} \le g + S x_0. \]

3. MPC for Humanoid Locomotion

Humanoid walking controllers use MPC on a simplified dynamic model, not on the full-body dynamics. The most common model is the Linear Inverted Pendulum Model (LIPM).

3.1 LIPM Dynamics

Assume constant CoM height \(h\). Then:

\[ \ddot{x} = \omega^2 (x - z), \qquad \omega = \sqrt{\frac{g}{h}}, \]

where:

  • \(x\): CoM horizontal position,
  • \(z\): ZMP (Zero Moment Point).

The ZMP acts as the control input.

In state-space form:

\[ X = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ \dot{x} \end{bmatrix}, \quad X_{k+1} = A X_k + B z_k. \]

3.2 MPC cost

The MPC minimizes CoM tracking error and ZMP effort:

\[ J = \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} \left[ (X_{k+i} - X_{k+i}^{ref})^\top Q (X_{k+i} - X_{k+i}^{ref}) + (z_{k+i} - z_{k+i}^{ref})^\top R (z_{k+i} - z_{k+i}^{ref}) \right] + X_{k+N}^\top P X_{k+N}. \]

3.3 ZMP constraints

The ZMP must remain inside the support polygon:

\[ z_{k+i} \in \mathcal{Z}_{\text{support}(i)} = \{ z \mid F_z z \le f_z \}. \]

This ensures dynamic balance.

3.4 MPC outputs

The MPC provides smooth trajectories for:

  • CoM position, velocity, acceleration
  • Torso orientation
  • Foot placements and swing trajectories

These references are fed into the Whole-Body QP.

4. Whole-Body Control Using Quadratic Programming (WBQP)

The full rigid-body dynamics of a humanoid robot is:

\[ M(q)\dot{v} + h(q,v) = S^\top \tau + J_c^\top f , \]

where:

  • \(q\): configuration,
  • \(v\): joint velocities,
  • \(f\): contact forces,
  • \(\tau\): joint torques,
  • \(J_c\): contact Jacobian.

4.1 Optimization variables

\[ x = \begin{bmatrix} \dot{v} \\ f \\ \tau \end{bmatrix}. \]

4.2 Objective function

Tasks are expressed as accelerations:

  • CoM tracking: \[ J_{CoM} \dot{v} \approx \ddot{x}_{CoM}^{ref}, \]
  • Foot swing trajectories: \[ J_{foot} \dot{v} \approx \ddot{x}_{foot}^{ref}. \]

Complete cost:

\[ \min_{\dot{v}, f, \tau} \| J_{CoM} \dot{v} - \ddot{x}_{CoM}^{ref} \|_w^2 + \| J_{foot} \dot{v} - \ddot{x}_{foot}^{ref} \|_w^2 + \| \tau \|_\lambda^2. \]

4.3 Constraints

Dynamic consistency

\[ M(q)\dot{v} + h(q,v) = S^\top \tau + J_c^\top f . \]

Contact constraints

\[ J_c \dot{v} = -\dot{J}_c v. \]

Friction cones

\[ \sqrt{f_x^2 + f_y^2} \le \mu f_z, \qquad f_z \ge 0. \]

Joint and torque limits

\[ |\tau_i| \le \tau_i^{max}. \]

The output is joint torques \(\tau^{*}\) or desired accelerations \(\dot{v}^{*}\).

5. Complete Humanoid Locomotion Architecture

A modern humanoid uses the following layered control system:

User velocity command
↓
Footstep planner (1–10 Hz)
↓
MPC locomotion (50–200 Hz)
↓
Whole-Body QP controller (200–1000 Hz)
↓
Low-level torque control (500–2000 Hz)

Each layer has a specific role:

  • Footstep planner: chooses future foot placements
  • MPC: stabilizes CoM, controls balance, anticipates future steps
  • Whole-Body QP: enforces full dynamics, contacts, friction, torques
  • Low-level control: applies torques to hardware

This architecture is used in Atlas, HRP-4, Talos, Valkyrie, Digit, and many research robots.

Conclusion

Model Predictive Control is a cornerstone of modern robotic locomotion. By combining anticipative high-level MPC (on simplified models) with fast whole-body QP controllers (on full-body dynamics), humanoid robots achieve stable, natural, and robust walking behavior.

This layered optimization architecture is one of the most successful control paradigms in robotics today, and remains an active area of research, particularly as reinforcement learning and learned models begin to augment or replace traditional components.
